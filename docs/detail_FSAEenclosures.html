FSAE Electrical Enclosures

Designed, tested, and validated custom electronics enclosures that are light and protect against, dust, water and EMI.

Responsibilities

  • Subteam Lead
  • Designer
  • Manufacturing and Testing

One of the goals for the MY18 vehicle was to decrease its mass and ensure the reliability of the electrical system. As such, the electrical enclosures were designed, tested and validated to ensure that they met numerous requirements such as, accesibility, dust and water ingress protected, EMI shielding, well mounted and easy to service.

The enclosures were machined from aluminum billet to a wall thickness of 0.050". The use of steel PEM inserts allows part count to be minimized, eases serviceability and makes the enclosures lighter than the previous year's 3D-printed enclosures. Enclosures are sealed using O-rings, aluminum tape, and waterjet-cut lids. Below is a series of images that outlines the process.

The process began by first exploring the potential ways of providing dust and water ingress protection to the enclosures. The team had used gaskets in the past, but that required a large number of bolts to be used around the enclosures perimeter. Attention then shifted towards O-rings, given that they are cheap, easy to replace and can be made for numerous applications. We decided to go forward with O-rings since a aluminum enclosure with a waterjet-cut lid would provide a static face seal. The Parker O-ring handbook was utilized to determine the best O-ring for our application, along with the recommended amount of squeeze and O-ring gland profile. A prototype was machined to validate the design and it turned out to be successful.

Preliminary tests done in order to validate the O-ring gland design.

The next steps in the process involved looking into EMI shielding. This was achieved by creating a "wand" that, when connected to an oscilloscope generates a wave that can be observed when placed next to a source of EMI. Various thicknesses of aluminum were tested in order to determine the minimum wall thickness required to attenuate the EMI. A sheet of 0.020" was found to attenuate the noise by ten-fold. This was used in conjuction with testing our machining requirements in order to determine the minimum wall thickness.
